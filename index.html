Food Access Analysis: Camden County, NJ

Authors: Vincent Lu & Ahmad Shahbaz
Course: Command-Line GIS (Rutgers 34:970:595, Fall 2025)

1. Project Overview

This project examines "Food Deserts" in Camden County—areas defined by the USDA as having both low income and low access to nutritious food. By overlaying demographic and transit data, we attempt to understand the characteristics of these communities.

4. Data Description

Datasets Used

USDA Food Access Research Atlas

  • Source: U.S. Department of Agriculture Economic Research Service
  • Year: 2019
  • Original format: GeoJSON with tract-level food desert classifications
  • Processing: Filtered to Camden County, NJ; joined with Census tract geometries

Census Demographic Data

  • Source: American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year Estimates (2015-2019)
  • Prepared by: U.S. Census Bureau
  • Original format: CSV accessed via API (tidycensus and cenpy libraries)
  • Variables: Median household income, race demographics, poverty rates, vehicle ownership
  • Processing: Joined to Census tract geometries; calculated Coefficient of Variation (CV) to flag unreliable estimates (CV ≥ 40)

NJ Transit Bus Stop Data

  • Source: NJ Transit Open Data
  • Original format: Point data (latitude/longitude coordinates)
  • Processing: Performed spatial join with Census tract polygons to calculate bus stop density per tract

Food Retail Locations

  • Source: OpenStreetMap via osmnx library
  • Last accessed: Fall 2025
  • Original format: Point data with retail tags
  • Processing: Queried and aggregated grocery stores, supermarkets, and convenience stores within Camden County boundaries

Data Quality Issues

  • Missing or low-confidence Census estimates (CV ≥ 40) were symbolized with hatching patterns or excluded from analysis
  • OpenStreetMap data completeness varies by neighborhood and may underrepresent smaller retailers
  • Transit stop density does not account for service frequency or route quality

5. Key Findings

  • Correlation: There is a strong correlation between food desert status and poverty rate in Camden County.
  • Demographics: Food deserts in this region have a significantly higher percentage of Black residents compared to non-desert tracts.
  • Transit Paradox: While the urban center has the highest density of bus stops, it also contains the most food deserts, suggesting that physical proximity to transit does not guarantee food security.
