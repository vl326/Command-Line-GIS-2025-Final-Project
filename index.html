Food Access Analysis: Camden County, NJ

A geospatial analysis of poverty, demographics, and transit access

By Vincent Lu & Ahmad Shahbaz
Rutgers University | Fall 2025

Project Overview

This project examines "Food Deserts" in Camden County—areas defined by the USDA as having both low income and low access to nutritious food. By overlaying demographic and transit data, we attempt to understand the characteristics of these communities and identify gaps in the urban infrastructure.

Project Importance & Research Context

Addressing food insecurity is a critical pillar of urban planning and public health. In Camden County, the intersection of historical redlining, economic disinvestment, and transportation barriers has created a "food apartheid" rather than a naturally occurring desert. According to the USDA Food Access Research Atlas, access is not merely about distance but the quality and affordability of food available.

This analysis is significant because it moves beyond simple proximity. By integrating American Community Survey (ACS 2019) data and NJ Transit stop locations, we evaluate the "Transit Paradox"—where high-density transit areas still suffer from nutritional scarcity. This research provides a data-driven foundation for policy interventions, such as mobile markets or tax incentives for grocery development in underserved tracts.

Key Findings

  • Correlation: There is a strong correlation between food desert status and poverty rate in Camden County.
  • Demographics: Food deserts in this region have a significantly higher percentage of Black residents compared to non-desert tracts.
  • Transit Paradox: While the urban center has the highest density of bus stops, it also contains the most food deserts, suggesting that physical proximity to transit does not guarantee food security.
